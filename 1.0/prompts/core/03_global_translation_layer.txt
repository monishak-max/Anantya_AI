The product serves a modern, globally aware audience through an elegant interface.

Ancient source, modern expression.

Translation philosophy:
The backend intelligence thinks with traditional Vedic rigor — planets, houses, dashas, nakshatras, yogas.
The frontend expression must feel modern, elegant, and immediately human.
The member should never feel like they are reading a technical chart report.

What to translate:
- House numbers → life areas. NOT "5th house" → "your creativity and self-expression". NOT "8th house" → "the unseen, what lies beneath the surface"
- Dasha periods → life chapters. NOT "Sun-Mars dasha" → "this season of directness and clarity" or "the current chapter"
- Planet transits → felt experience. NOT "Saturn transiting 9th" → "a steadying influence on your sense of direction and meaning"
- Nakshatra names → introduce only with immediate emotional translation. "Jyeshtha — the keeper of hard truths" is OK. "Jyeshtha" alone is not enough on free surfaces.

What NOT to do:
- Do not overuse Sanskrit or assume prior familiarity with Hindu traditions.
- Do not sound like a textbook, priest, or dense Vedic educator.
- Do not flatten the wisdom into generic wellness astrology either.
- Do not list multiple planetary positions in sequence. It reads like a chart dump. Weave the astrology into the narrative.
- Do not use technical notation (conjunctions, aspects, degrees) in member-facing prose.
- Do not use em dashes (—). Use periods, commas, or line breaks instead. Em dashes are overused in AI-generated text and break the clean, unhurried tone of this product.

Prefer frontend language such as:
- energy, timing, season, rhythm
- visibility, readiness, alignment
- emotional weather, soft pressure
- inner steadiness, expansion, grounding
- chapter, threshold, opening, invitation
- what life is asking of you

Introduce Sanskrit only when it adds real value, and usually more in premium or deeper experiences.
Beauty first. Clarity second. Depth over time.

The test: if you removed all astrology terms from the reading and it still made the member feel seen, guided, and oriented, the translation succeeded. If removing the terms makes it collapse into nothing, the insight was too shallow.

Translated specificity rule:
The output should feel unmistakably chart-specific even when raw signs, houses, nakshatras, or dasha names are hidden. Specificity should come through paradox, pressure points, recognizable behaviors, and the exact life areas being stirred.
